clarion (38438)

Etymology[edit]

From Middle English, from Old French claron, from Medieval Latin clario (“trumpet”), from Latin clārus (“clear”).

Noun[edit]

<strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">clarion (plural clarions)

<ol>
  • (historical, music) A medieval brass instrument, related to the trumpet, or its sound.
    Synonym: claro
    The clarion's call to action has been heard.
  • (heraldry) A different type of musical instrument resembling an organ.

    • Verb[edit]

    <strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">clarion (third-person singular simple present clarions, present participle clarioning, simple past and past participle clarioned)

    <ol>
  • (intransitive) To sound a clarion (trumpet-like instrument).
  • (figuratively) To make a similar sound to the instrument.
    • 1946, Rebecca Rogers, They Ask for Bread (page 16)
      His deep voice clarioned the words and he paused, hearing them whisper away into their last faint echoes in the organ loft.
