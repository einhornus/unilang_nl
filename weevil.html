weevil (41283)


Etymology

From Middle English wevel, from Old English ang">wifel (“beetle”), from Proto-Germanic *wibilaz, from Proto-Indo-European *webʰel-, from *(h₁)webʰ- (“to wave, to weave”), said to be from the woven appearance of a weevil’s larval case,[1] + *-el-, *-l̥- (diminutive or attributive suffix); see also wave and weave.

Compare Saxon">Old Saxon goldwivel, wivel (“glowworm”); Middle Low German wevel; High German">Old High German goh">wibil, goh">wipil (modern German Wiebel (“beetle; chafer”)); Lithuanian vãbalas (“beetle; weevil”); Old Norse non">vifill, as in non">tordyfill (“dung beetle, scarab”) (whence Dutch tortwevel; Icelandic tordýfill, Norwegian tordivel, Old English ang">tordwifel, Swedish tordyvel); dialectal Russian веблица (véblica, “intestinal worm”).

Noun

weevil (plural weevils)

  1. Any of several small herbivorous beetles in the superfamily Curculionoidea, many having a distinctive snout.
  2. Any of several small herbivorous beetles in the family Curculionidae belonging to the superfamily Curculionoidea.
  3. Any of several similar but more distantly related beetles such as the biscuit weevil">biscuit weevil (Stegobium paniceum).
  4. (figuratively, derogatory) A loathsome person.
