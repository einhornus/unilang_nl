Etymology 1

From Middle English mingen, mengen, from Old English ang">mengan (“to mix, combine, unite, associate with, consort, cohabit with, disturb, converse”), from Proto-Germanic *mangijaną (“to mix, knead”), from Proto-Indo-European *menk- (“to rumple, knead”). Cognate with Dutch mengen (“to mix, blend, mingle”), German mengen (“to mix”), Danish mænge (“to rub”), Old English ang">ġemang (“mixture, union, troop, crowd, multitude, congregation, assembly, business, cohabitation”). More at among.

Verb

ming (third-person singular simple present mings, present participle minging, simple past and past participle past|and|past|ptcp-form-of" lang="en">minged or past|and|past|ptcp-form-of" lang="en">meint or past|and|past|ptcp-form-of" lang="en">ment)

  1. (now rare) To mix, blend, mingle.
    • 1562, William Turner, Baths:
      I founde here and there litle peces of marquesites and stones, menged with copper, but I could by no sense or wit perceyue, that the bathes had any notable qualitie thereof.
  2. (obsolete) To bring (people, animals etc.) together; to be joined, in marriage or sexual intercourse.
    • 1590, Edmund Spenser, The Faerie Queene, I.ii:
      the old man [...] him brought into a secret part, / Where that false couple were full closely ment / In wanton lust and lewd embracement [...].
  3. (Great Britain">Britain, dialectal) To produce through mixing; especially, to knead.

Synonyms

Etymology 3

From Middle English mingen, mengen, mungen, muneȝen, from Old English ang">myngian, ang">mynegian, ang">ġemynegian (“to bring to mind, have in mind”), from ang">myne (“mind”), from ang">ġemunan (“to remember”), from Proto-Germanic *munaną (“to think”), from Proto-Indo-European *men- (“to think”). Merged in Middle English with Old English ang">ġemyndgian (“to remember, be mindful, remind, intend, commemorate, mention, exhort, impel, warn, demand payment”). More at mind.
