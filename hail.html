Etymology 1

From Middle English hayle, haile, hail, from Old English ang">hæġl, hæġel, from Proto-Germanic *haglaz (compare West Frisian heil, Low German Hagel, Dutch hagel, German Hagel, Danish hagl). Either from Proto-Indo-European *kagʰlos (“pebble”), or from *ḱoḱló-, a reduplication of *ḱel- (“cold”) (compare Old Norse non">héla (“frost”)).

Root-cognates outside of Germanic include Welsh language">Welsh caill (“testicle”), Breton kell (“testicle”), Lithuanian šešėlis (“shade, shadow”), Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek κάχληξ (kákhlēx, “pebble”), Albanian çakëll (“pebble”), Sanskrit शिशिर (śíśira, “cool, cold”).

Noun

hail (uncountable)

  1. Balls or pieces of ice falling as precipitation, often in connection with a thunderstorm.

Verb

hail (third-person singular simple present hails, present participle hailing, simple past and past participle past|and|past|ptcp-form-of" lang="en">hailed)

  1. (impersonal) Of hail, to fall from the sky.
    They say it's going to hail tomorrow.
  2. (intransitive) To send or release hail.
    The cloud would hail down furiously within a few minutes.
  3. To pour down in rapid succession.

Etymology 2

From Middle English heil (“healthy, sound”), from Old Norse non">heill, from Proto-Germanic *hailaz (“whole, entire, healthy”). The verb is from Middle English heilen, itself from the adjective. Doublet of whole and hale.

Adjective

hail (comparative hailer, superlative hailest)

  1. (obsolete) Healthy, whole, safe.
